**Upcoming Event**

**A Musical Lecture and Presentation by Renowned Violinist Siqing Lu**

**“The Beauty of the Violin”**

** Date & Time**: Thursday, February 19, 2026 | 4:00 PM – 5:30 PM

** Location:** Fordham University, Lincoln Center Campus, Gabelli School of Business, Tiered Lecture Hall 333 (140 West 62nd Street, New York, NY).

**A reception will follow the presentation.**

*Sponsored by:*The Department of Languages and Cultures at Fordham University

The Department of Art History and Music at Fordham University

The Strategic Research Consortium on Global Studies at Fordham University

** About the Speaker**: Siqing Lu is one of the most influential and acclaimed contemporary Chinese violinists. He currently serves as Principal of the Yehudi Menuhin School Qingdao, Artistic Director of the National Centre for the Performing Arts (NCPA) “May Festival” in Beijing, and Lifetime Artistic Ambassador for the Melbourne Symphony Orchestra. As the first Asian violinist to win the Gold Prize at the prestigious Paganini International Violin Competition in Italy, Mr. Lu has maintained a prolific presence on the world’s leading musical stages. His career spans performances at the Musikverein in Vienna, Lincoln Center and Carnegie Hall in New York, and Suntory Hall in Tokyo. He has collaborated with many of the world’s finest ensembles, including the Philharmonic Orchestra, the London Symphony Orchestra, the San Francisco Symphony, the Melbourne Symphony Orchestra, and the Hong Kong Philharmonic Orchestra. Celebrated by international media as an “outstandingly talented violinist,” Siqing Lu has earned global acclaim across nearly fifty countries for his passionate interpretations and unparalleled musical charisma.

** Lecture Synopsis**: Centering on the theme “How the Violin Transcends Culture and Time to Express Emotion,” this lecture explores the violin’s historical origins, craftsmanship, and tonal qualities. Through a combination of live commentary and curated video analysis, Mr. Lu will guide the audience through the world of violin artistry. His presentation will include Western masterpieces by composers such as Paganini, Massenet, and Monti, along with the iconic Chinese masterpiece, the Butterfly Lovers Violin Concerto. Drawing on his extensive international career, Mr. Lu will share personal reflections on music education, cultural heritage, and artistic growth, while discussing the enduring value and significance of classical music in contemporary life.

Designed for anyone interested in music, art, culture, and the humanities, this lecture requires no prior musical training. We invite you to experience the profound and timeless allure of classical music as presented by this violin virtuoso and global ambassador of transcultural forms.

**Past Events**

**2025. 04. 07 – Workshop on R for Data Analysis: Introduction to R – Essential Data Analysis and Statistical Techniques with RStudio Cloud**This workshop, led by Dr. Debbie Huang, a psychiatric epidemiologist with expertise in mental health disparities and digital research methods, offered participants a practical and accessible introduction to data analysis using the R programming language. Tailored for beginners, the session guided attendees through the foundational steps of working with R in RStudio Cloud, a browser-based platform ideal for those new to coding environments. Participants learned how to import and manage datasets, apply descriptive statistical methods, conduct bivariate analyses, and perform simple regression modeling. The workshop combined theoretical instruction with hands-on exercises. By the end of the session, attendees were equipped with essential skills in R to support future data-driven research projects. This workshop served as an important entry point for faculty, students, and researchers interested in strengthening their quantitative analysis capabilities through open-source tools like R.

**2025. 03 .07 and 03. 28 – Workshop on Scoping and Systematic Reviews: Building Competency in Evidence Synthesis**This two-session workshop was led by Dr. Xiao Ding, a postdoctoral scholar at Case Western Reserve University, and Elle Covington, an evidence synthesis librarian at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln. It offered participants a comprehensive, step-by-step introduction to conducting rigorous and transparent evidence synthesis. Designed for researchers at all levels, the workshop focused on scoping and systematic reviews, as well as related synthesis methods such as meta-analyses and content analyses. In Session I, participants were introduced to the fundamentals of evidence synthesis, including selecting appropriate review types, formulating research questions, developing search strategies, and understanding key guidelines such as PRISMA, Cochrane, and Prospero. Session II provided hands-on training, allowing attendees to practice database searching, screening studies, coding data, and using tools like Zotero, Rayyan, and Covidence to support a replicable and organized review process. The workshop emphasized both theoretical knowledge and applied skills. Together, they provided practical guidance to help participants strengthen their evidence synthesis skills and enhance the transparency and quality of their literature reviews.

**2023. 12. 07 – Fordham Research Forum on Global Studies: Global in the Local – Socioeconomic Inclusion and Community Empowerment**

This forum explored the global-local nexus in socioeconomic inclusion and community empowerment. The event opened with a welcome by Dr. Fuhua Zhai (Co-Director of the Fordham Strategic Research Consortium on Global Studies) and remarks by Dr. George Hong (Fordham University Chief Research Officer). The keynote address, delivered by Dr. Hirokazu Yoshikawa (NYU), titled *“Forced Displacement, Participatory Action, and Community Empowerment: Learning from Rohingya Children and Caregivers,”* set the stage for deeper exploration into global displacement and empowerment efforts. A roundtable discussion moderated by Dr. Yuko Miki (Co-Director of the Fordham Strategic Research Consortium on Global Studies) followed, featuring contributions from Dr. Sarah Eltantawi (Fordham Theology) and Dr. Sarah Lockhart (Fordham Political Science). The event also featured a luncheon and a panel forum moderated by Dr. Aseel Sawalha (Co-Director of the Fordham Strategic Research Consortium on Global Studies). Panelists included Dr. Sameena Azhar (Fordham Graduate School of Social Service) on the stigma faced by Khwaja Sira communities in Pakistan, Dr. Tyesha Maddox (Fordham African and African American Studies) on mutual aid strategies among Black immigrants, Dr. Sophie Mitra (Fordham Economics) on disability rights research, and Dr. Fadi Skeiker (Fordham Theatre) on applied theatre for refugee empowerment. The forum concluded with closing remarks from Dr. Ji Seon Lee (Fordham University Associate Provost for Faculty Affairs).

**2022. 04. 11 – Lecture on Bloody Sunday**

Ms. Ciara O’Connor-Pozo, Project Coordinator for the Bloody Sunday Trust in Derry, Ireland, delivered a lecture reflecting on the 50th anniversary of Bloody Sunday, where 14 civil rights marchers were killed by British soldiers. The session highlighted ongoing efforts for justice and truth in Ireland’s "Troubles," emphasizing the continued struggle to address state violence. The event was followed by a Q&A session, allowing attendees to further explore these historical events and their lasting impacts.

**2021. 12. 03 – Meeting Points: Materials, Technologies, Communities**

Professor Abeer Seikaly (Yale University) led this in-person event, sharing her interdisciplinary work on architecture, memory, and cultural empowerment. Seikaly’s designs, which aim to redefine how communities engage with space, have garnered international recognition. **2021. 11. 18 – Affective Scales of the Anthropocene: Gardening at the End of the World**

Dr. Molly Doane (University of Illinois, Chicago) presented her ongoing research on environmental politics, climate refugees, and social movements in Mexico and the United States. Her talk, held in person, was based on her book *Stealing Shining Rivers*, which explores NGO-led conservation efforts in Oaxaca, Mexico. Dr. Doane’s work provided insights into how local autonomy is negotiated within mainstream environmental projects, touching on the broader implications of the Anthropocene and environmental conservation movements.

**2021. 10. 22 – Syrian Refugees and the Politics of Humanitarianism**

Dr. Yasser Munif (Emerson College) delivered a Zoom lecture focusing on the intersections of colonial history, racial identities, and the politics of humanitarianism concerning Syrian refugees. Drawing on the Foucauldian and Fanonian perspectives, Dr. Munif explored how French colonialism shaped urban spaces to influence identities and lived experiences in both colonial territories and France. His research combined archival and ethnographic investigations, focusing on the transference of knowledge within the colonial contexts.
